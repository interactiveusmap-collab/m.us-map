Overview

Illinois, the "Prairie State" and "Land of Lincoln," is the cultural and industrial anchor of the Midwest. Boasting Chicago as a world-class global city alongside fertile farmland, it leads in finance, logistics, and agricultural innovation.

Historical Significance

Illinois officially joined the Union on December 3, 1818, as the 21st state. It transformed from a rugged frontier into a pivotal Union stronghold during the Civil War and later into the industrial heart of America.

Key Landmarks & Economy

Willis Tower:

Formerly the Sears Tower, this iconic skyscraper remains a global symbol of Chicago's skyline.

Lincoln Home:

The National Historic Site in Springfield where Abraham Lincoln lived, preserved as a window into the 16th President's life.

Agriculture & Tech:

Illinois is a global leader in corn and soybean production while hosting massive tech hubs like Fermilab.

Did You Know?

  • Illinois was the first state to ratify the 13th Amendment to the U.S. Constitution, which abolished slavery.
  • The state is home to Cahokia Mounds, the largest prehistoric Native American settlement north of Mexico.
  • The world's first-ever skyscraper, the Home Insurance Building, was built in Chicago in 1885.
