Overview

Nebraska, the heart of the Great Plains, is a global leader in corn and beef production. Its vast landscapes once guided pioneers along the Platte River, and today it thrives as a center for finance, insurance, and agriculture.

Historical Significance

Admitted on March 1, 1867, as the 37th state, Nebraska was a critical gateway for westward expansion, serving as the primary corridor for the Oregon, Mormon, and California trails.

Top Cities & Hubs

The state's bustling economic motor, globally recognized for its financial services, rail transport, and top-tier zoo.

The state capital and educational heart, home to the University of Nebraska and the nation's only unicameral legislature.

3

Bellevue

Population: ~63,000

Nebraska's oldest city and a vital defense hub, adjacent to Offutt Air Force Base and the US Strategic Command.

4

Population: ~53,000

A regional powerhouse for trade and manufacturing in central Nebraska, famous for its heritage and sandhill crane migration.

5

Kearney

Population: ~34,000

Strategically located on the historic trails, it is a bustling hub for higher education, medicine, and regional transit.

Key Landmarks & Economy

Chimney Rock:

The most famous landmark on the Oregon Trail, this spire of rock symbolized hope and progress for thousands of pioneers.

Carhenge:

A unique quirky replica of England's Stonehenge fashioned from vintage automobiles in the western Nebraska high plains.

Henry Doorly Zoo:

Omaha's world-renowned conservation center, consistently ranked as one of the best and largest zoos on the planet.

Did You Know?

  • Nebraska is the home of **Arbor Day**, first celebrated in 1872 to encourage tree planting.
  • **Kool-Aid** was invented in Hastings, Nebraska, in 1927 by Edwin Perkins.
  • It is the only state in the US with a **unicameral** (one-house) state legislature.
