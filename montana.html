Overview

Montana, known as 'Big Sky Country,' is a sanctuary of raw natural splendor. From the jagged peaks of the Rockies to vast rolling plains, its beauty is matched by a robust economy in mineral extraction and world-class mountain tourism.

Historical Significance

Joining the Union on November 8, 1889, as the 41st state, Montana solidified the American western frontier and became an essential contributor to the nation's rich mineral and agricultural legacy.

Top Cities & Hubs

1

The largest city and 'Magic City' of the plains, serving as the region's primary trade, medical, and energy hub.

2

Missoula

Population: ~75,000

A vibrant cultural and intellectual center, home to the University of Montana and surrounded by five mountain ranges.

3

Great Falls

Population: ~60,000

Named for the massive waterfalls on the Missouri River, it is a key site for air defense and regional logistics.

4

Bozeman

Population: ~56,000

One of the nation's fastest-growing tech and outdoor recreation hubs, serving as the main gateway to Yellowstone.

5

Butte

Population: ~35,000

Once the 'Richest Hill on Earth,' it holds a legendary status for its massive copper mining heritage and historic district.

Key Landmarks & Economy

Glacier National Park:

The 'Crown of the Continent,' featuring jagged peaks, alpine meadows, and over 700 miles of wilderness trails.

Little Bighorn:

A national monument preserving the site of the historic 1876 battle, a solemn reminder of Western frontier history.

Helena Capital:

A historic gold camp that became the state’s political center, boasting grand architecture and Rocky Mountain views.

Did You Know?

  • Montana has the **largest migratory elk herd** in the nation.
  • The state's name is derived from the Spanish word **'montaña'**, meaning mountain.
  • At Glacier Nat. Park, you can stand at a spot called **Triple Divide Peak**, where water flows to three different oceans.
