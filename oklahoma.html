Overview

Oklahoma, the 'Sooner State,' is a heartland gem defined by its rolling plains, vast energy reserves, and deep-rooted Native American heritage. A critical hub for aerospace and energy, it offers a unique blend of pioneering history and modern industrial growth.

Historical Significance

Oklahoma officially joined the Union on November 16, 1907, as the 46th state. Its admission followed the historic Land Runs of the 1880s and '90s, and its identity remains forever shaped by the 39 tribal nations that call the state home.

Top Cities & Hubs

1

Oklahoma City

Population: ~690,000

The state capital and largest city, renowned for its massive energy sector, livestock markets, and the vibrant Bricktown district.

2

Tulsa

Population: ~410,000

The former 'Oil Capital of the World,' today it is a flourishing center for aerospace, the arts, and stunning Art Deco architecture.

3

Norman

Population: ~128,000

A premier academic city, home to the University of Oklahoma and the National Weather Center, a global hub for meteorology.

4

Broken Arrow

Population: ~115,000

A thriving suburb of Tulsa that has become a major regional center for manufacturing and high-quality residential living.

5

Edmond

Population: ~95,000

A prestigious community north of Oklahoma City, recognized for its top-tier schools, parks, and the University of Central Oklahoma.

Key Landmarks & Economy

National Cowboy Museum:

A world-class institution in Oklahoma City that preserves the iconic art, history, and lore of the American West.

First Americans Museum:

A spectacular museum in OKC that honors the shared experiences and cultures of the 39 tribal nations in Oklahoma.

Philbrook Museum of Art:

An Italianate villa in Tulsa surrounded by 25 acres of lush gardens, housing an extraordinary collection of global art.

Did You Know?

  • The **first parking meter** in the world was installed in Oklahoma City in 1935.
  • Oklahoma has more **man-made lakes** than any other state in the country.
  • The name 'Oklahoma' comes from the Choctaw words 'okla' and 'humma,' meaning **'red people.'**
